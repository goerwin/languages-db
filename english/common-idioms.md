---
title: English Common Idioms
description: Widely used English idiomatic expressions with definitions and example sentences showing how they are used in everyday conversation
---

# Bite the bullet

def: To decide to do something difficult or unpleasant that one has been putting off or hesitating over
eg: I hate going to the dentist, but I'll just have to bite the bullet and make an appointment
eg: The company had to bite the bullet and lay off several employees to stay afloat

# Break a leg

def: A way to wish someone good luck, especially before a performance
eg: You have your big audition today! Break a leg!
eg: I'm so nervous about my speech. - Don't worry, you'll be great. Break a leg!

# Call it a day

def: To stop what you are doing because you do not want to do any more or think you have done enough
eg: We've been working on this report for ten hours; let's call it a day and finish it tomorrow
eg: After the third attempt failed, they decided to call it a day

# Cut corners

def: To do something in the easiest, cheapest, or fastest way, often at the expense of quality or safety
eg: They cut corners when building the bridge, and now it’s unsafe
eg: Don't cut corners on your research paper; the professor will notice

# Get out of hand

def: To become difficult or impossible to control
eg: The party got out of hand when the neighbors called the police
eg: Inflation has really gotten out of hand this year

# Hit the sack / Hit the hay

def: To go to bed in order to sleep
eg: It’s been a long day. I’m going to hit the sack
eg: We have an early flight tomorrow, so we should hit the hay soon

# It's not rocket science

def: Used to say that something is not difficult to do or understand
eg: Just plug it in and turn it on. It's not rocket science
eg: Managing your budget isn't rocket science; you just need to track your spending

# Let someone off the hook

def: To allow someone to escape from a difficult situation or to avoid being punished
eg: I was supposed to do the dishes, but my brother let me off the hook and did them for me
eg: The police officer let him off the hook with just a warning instead of a ticket

# Miss the boat

def: To be too late to take an opportunity
eg: I wanted to buy tickets for the concert, but I missed the boat; they're sold out
eg: If you don't apply for the scholarship now, you might miss the boat

# No pain, no gain

def: You have to work hard for something you want
eg: My muscles are so sore from the workout, but no pain, no gain!
eg: Studying for hours is exhausting, but no pain, no gain if you want to pass the bar exam

# On the ball

def: To be quick to understand and react to things; alert and efficient
eg: Our new assistant is really on the ball; she anticipates problems before they happen
eg: You need to be on the ball to trade in the stock market

# Pull someone's leg

def: To tease someone or try to make them believe something that is not true as a joke
eg: I didn't really win the lottery. I was just pulling your leg
eg: Don't take him seriously; he's probably just pulling your leg

# Speak of the devil

def: Said when a person appears just after being mentioned
eg: We were just talking about Mike, and speak of the devil, here he is!
eg: Did you hear that Sarah is moving? Oh, speak of the devil, she’s calling me right now

# That's the last straw

def: The latest problem in a series of problems that makes a situation impossible to accept
eg: She’s been late every day this week, but missing the client meeting was the last straw
eg: When the car broke down again, it was the last straw; I decided to sell it

# The best of both worlds

def: A situation in which you can enjoy the advantages of two very different things at the same time
eg: Working part-time allows me to earn money and spend time with my kids—it's the best of both worlds
eg: This tablet laptop gives you the portability of a tablet and the power of a laptop; it's the best of both worlds

# Time flies when you're having fun

def: Time seems to pass very quickly when you are enjoying yourself
eg: Is it already midnight? Time flies when you're having fun!
eg: The vacation was over before we knew it. Time flies when you're having fun

# To get bent out of shape

def: To become angry, upset, or offended, often about something minor
eg: Don't get bent out of shape just because I forgot to call
eg: He got all bent out of shape when he wasn't invited to the meeting

# To make matters worse

def: To make a bad situation even more unpleasant or difficult
eg: We were already late, and to make matters worse, it started raining
eg: He lost his keys, and to make matters worse, his phone battery died

# Under the weather

def: Feeling sick or not well
eg: I'm feeling a bit under the weather today, so I think I'll stay home
eg: She missed the party because she was under the weather

# We'll cross that bridge when we come to it

def: To delay worrying about a possible problem until it actually happens
eg: What if the venue is booked? - We'll cross that bridge when we come to it
eg: Don't worry about the cost of college yet; he's only five. We'll cross that bridge when we come to it

# A blessing in disguise

def: A bad situation that eventually results in something good
eg: Missing the bus was a blessing in disguise because I met my future wife at the stop
eg: Losing my job was a blessing in disguise; it pushed me to start my own business

# Beat around the bush

def: To avoid talking about what is important or the main point
eg: Stop beating around the bush and tell me what you really want
eg: He beat around the bush for ten minutes before asking for a raise

# Better late than never

def: It is better for someone to arrive or do something late than not to arrive or do it at all
eg: I finally learned to swim at age 30—better late than never
eg: The report is three days overdue, but better late than never

# Break the ice

def: To make people who have not met before feel more relaxed and comfortable
eg: He told a joke to break the ice at the start of the meeting
eg: Playing a game is a great way to break the ice at a party

# Costs an arm and a leg

def: To be very expensive
eg: I want that car, but it costs an arm and a leg
eg: Fixing the roof is going to cost an arm and a leg

# Give someone the benefit of the doubt

def: To believe something good about someone, rather than something bad, when you have the possibility of doing either
eg: I'm not sure if he's telling the truth, but I'll give him the benefit of the doubt
eg: She was late, but let's give her the benefit of the doubt; maybe there was traffic

# Hit the nail on the head

def: To describe exactly what is causing a situation or problem; to be exactly right
eg: You really hit the nail on the head with that analysis
eg: She hit the nail on the head when she said the problem was lack of communication

# Let the cat out of the bag

def: To reveal a secret carelessly or by mistake
eg: It was supposed to be a surprise party, but my sister let the cat out of the bag
eg: Don't let the cat out of the bag about the engagement yet!

# Once in a blue moon

def: Very rarely
eg: I only see my cousins once in a blue moon because they live in Australia
eg: My dad cooks dinner once in a blue moon

# Piece of cake

def: Something that is very easy to do
eg: The exam was a piece of cake; I finished it in half the time
eg: Fixing this computer issue should be a piece of cake

# Pull yourself together

def: To recover control of one's emotions
eg: Stop crying and pull yourself together! We have work to do
eg: It took him a while to pull himself together after the bad news

# So far so good

def: Used to say that things have been going well up to this point
eg: How's the new project going? - So far so good.
eg: We've driven 300 miles and the car is running great—so far so good

# The elephant in the room

def: An obvious problem or difficult issue that people are avoiding discussing
eg: His drinking problem was the elephant in the room that no one wanted to mention
eg: We need to address the elephant in the room: the budget cuts

# Through thick and thin

def: Under all circumstances, no matter how difficult
eg: She stood by her husband through thick and thin
eg: Best friends stick together through thick and thin

# Under the table

def: Done secretly and usually illegally (often regarding money)
eg: He gets paid under the table to avoid paying taxes
eg: They made a deal under the table to secure the contract

# Wrap your head around something

def: To find a way to understand or accept something difficult or complex
eg: I can't wrap my head around why she would quit such a good job
eg: The physics concept was hard to wrap my head around at first

# You can say that again

def: Used to express strong agreement with what someone has just said
eg: This pizza is delicious! - You can say that again!
eg: It's so hot today - You can say that again

# Your guess is as good as mine

def: Used to say that you do not know the answer to a question
eg: When will the train arrive? - Your guess is as good as mine
eg: Why is the system down? - Your guess is as good as mine

# A penny for your thoughts?

def: Used to ask someone what they are thinking
eg: You've been quiet for a while. A penny for your thoughts?
eg: You look serious. A penny for your thoughts?

# Actions speak louder than words

def: What someone does is more important than what they say they will do
eg: He promised to help, but actions speak louder than words
eg: Don't just tell me you love me; show me. Actions speak louder than words

# To live up to the hype

def: To meet or exceed expectations that have been set for something or someone
eg: The movie was so anticipated that I doubted it could live up to the hype, but it was even better than I expected
eg: The restaurant didn't live up to the hype. The food was just average

# What is the catch?

def: Used when something seems too good to be true, and you suspect there is a hidden drawback or condition
eg: This phone plan is so cheap. What's the catch?
eg: The offer sounds great, but what’s the catch?

# Shoot yourself in the foot

def: To do something that causes problems or harm to yourself, often accidentally or through poor judgment
eg: He was shooting himself in the foot by arguing with his boss in front of everyone
eg: By forgetting to submit her application on time, she really shot herself in the foot

# Rough around the edges

def: Someone or something that is not perfect; having some flaws, lacking refinement, or still needing improvement
eg: The new employee is a bit rough around the edges, but he’s learning quickly
eg: The first draft of her novel was rough around the edges, but the story was very compelling

# Add insult to injury

def: To make a bad situation even worse
eg: First, I missed the bus, and then, to add insult to injury, it started to rain
eg: To add insult to injury, after firing him, they asked for the company car keys back immediately

# Barking up the wrong tree

def: To be looking for a solution in the wrong place or accusing the wrong person
eg: If you think I’m the one who broke the window, you’re barking up the wrong tree
eg: The investigators are barking up the wrong tree; the real culprit is already gone

# Burn bridges

def: To destroy relationships or opportunities, making it impossible to return to a previous situation
eg: Even if you hate your job, don't burn bridges when you leave; you might need a reference later
eg: He burned his bridges by insulting his former boss on social media

# Cut to the chase

def: To get to the point without wasting time
eg: We don’t have much time, so let’s cut to the chase: how much will this cost?
eg: Instead of a long introduction, the speaker decided to cut to the chase

# Face the music

def: To accept responsibility for something you have done
eg: I broke the vase, so now I have to go home and face the music
eg: After ignoring his taxes for years, he finally had to face the music

# Go the extra mile

def: To do more than is expected of you
eg: She’s a great employee who always goes the extra mile to help customers
eg: If you want to succeed, you need to go the extra mile

# Hang in there

def: Said to encourage someone who is going through a difficult time
eg: I know studying is tough, but hang in there; exams are almost over
eg: Hang in there! Things will get better soon

# Hit the road

def: To leave or start a journey
eg: It’s getting late; we should hit the road if we want to beat the traffic
eg: After a long visit, they finally hit the road back home

# Jump on the bandwagon

def: To join others in doing something that has become fashionable or popular
eg: When the team started winning, everyone wanted to jump on the bandwagon
eg: Many companies are jumping on the AI bandwagon now

# Kill two birds with one stone

def: To achieve two things with a single action
eg: I can kill two birds with one stone by dropping off the mail on my way to the grocery store
eg: Cycling to work kills two birds with one stone: you save money and get exercise

# Leave no stone unturned

def: To do everything possible to find something or solve a problem
eg: The police promised to leave no stone unturned in their search for the missing child
eg: We left no stone unturned looking for the source of the error

# On the fence

def: Unable or unwilling to make a decision or take a side
eg: I’m still on the fence about whether to buy the house or not
eg: He’s been on the fence for weeks regarding the job offer

# Play it by ear

def: To decide how to deal with a situation as it develops, rather than having a fixed plan
eg: We don't have a schedule for the trip; we're just going to play it by ear
eg: I'm not sure how long the meeting will last, so let's play it by ear for lunch

# Rule of thumb

def: A broadly accurate guide or principle, based on experience or practice rather than theory
eg: A good rule of thumb is to save 10% of your income
eg: As a rule of thumb, plant your garden after the last frost

# Sit tight

def: To wait patiently and take no action until you hear otherwise
eg: Just sit tight, and the doctor will be with you shortly
eg: We were told to sit tight while they processed our application

# Steal someone's thunder

def: To win praise for yourself by doing what someone else was going to do, or by revealing their idea first
eg: I was going to announce my promotion, but she stole my thunder by announcing hers first
eg: Don't steal the bride's thunder by wearing white to the wedding

# Take a rain check

def: Used to tell someone that you cannot accept an invitation now, but would like to do so at a later time
eg: I’m busy tonight, but can I take a rain check?
eg: He had to take a rain check on the dinner plans due to work

# Take it with a grain of salt

def: To not completely believe something that you are told, because you think it is unlikely to be true
eg: He tends to exaggerate, so take his stories with a grain of salt
eg: Read the reviews, but take them with a grain of salt as some are fake

# The ball is in your court

def: It is up to you to make the next decision or step
eg: I’ve made my offer; now the ball is in your court
eg: She sent the email, and now the ball is in his court to reply

# Throw in the towel

def: To quit or give up
eg: After hours of trying to fix the engine, he finally threw in the towel
eg: Don't throw in the towel just yet; you're almost finished

# Under the radar

def: Not detected or noticed by people
eg: He likes to keep his personal life under the radar
eg: This little restaurant has stayed under the radar, but the food is amazing

# Up in the air

def: Uncertain or not yet decided
eg: Our vacation plans are still up in the air until we get time off work
eg: The future of the project is up in the air right now

# Better for it

def: To be improved or to have gained some benefit as a result of a difficult or challenging experience
eg: The training was tough, but I'm better for it
eg: She went through a lot last year, but she's stronger and better for it now

# Back to the drawing board

def: To start over because the previous plan or attempt has failed
eg: The prototype didn't work, so it's back to the drawing board
eg: Our proposal was rejected, which means we're back to the drawing board

# Bite off more than you can chew

def: To take on a task or responsibility that is too much for you to handle
eg: I volunteered to organize the whole event by myself, and I think I bit off more than I could chew
eg: Be careful not to bite off more than you can chew with all those classes this semester

# By the skin of your teeth

def: Just barely; by a very narrow margin
eg: I passed the exam by the skin of my teeth—one more wrong answer and I would have failed
eg: We made it to the airport by the skin of our teeth; the gate was about to close

# Don't cry over spilled milk

def: Don't waste time worrying about things that have already happened and cannot be changed
eg: Yes, you made a mistake, but don't cry over spilled milk. Let's focus on fixing it
eg: The files were accidentally deleted, but there's no use crying over spilled milk; let's recover what we can

# Easy come, easy go

def: Something that is gained without much effort is easily lost or spent
eg: He won $500 at poker but lost it all the next night—easy come, easy go
eg: She quit her job after just a week. Easy come, easy go, I guess

# Get your act together

def: To organize yourself and start behaving more responsibly or effectively
eg: If you don't get your act together, you're going to fail this course
eg: The team needs to get their act together before the next game

# In the nick of time

def: Just in time; at the last possible moment before it is too late
eg: The firefighters arrived in the nick of time to save the building
eg: I submitted my application in the nick of time—just minutes before the deadline

# Nip it in the bud

def: To stop something at an early stage before it develops into something worse
eg: If you notice bad habits forming, it's best to nip them in the bud
eg: The manager nipped the conflict in the bud before it affected the whole team

# Off the top of my head

def: Without careful thought or investigation; from memory or as an immediate reaction
eg: Off the top of my head, I'd say there are about 30 people coming to the party
eg: I can't remember the exact number off the top of my head, but I'll check

# Go back to square one

def: To start something over again from the very beginning because a previous attempt failed
eg: The experiment failed, so we had to go back to square one
eg: When we realized our approach was wrong, we went back to square one

# See eye to eye

def: To agree with someone on something
eg: My sister and I don't always see eye to eye on politics
eg: The two managers finally see eye to eye on the new strategy

# Give the cold shoulder

def: To deliberately ignore someone or treat them in an unfriendly way
eg: Ever since our argument, she's been giving me the cold shoulder
eg: He tried to apologize, but she gave him the cold shoulder

# It takes two to tango

def: Both people involved in a situation are equally responsible for it
eg: You can't just blame him for the argument. It takes two to tango
eg: The project failed because neither side communicated well—it takes two to tango

# Let sleeping dogs lie

def: To avoid interfering in a situation that is currently causing no problems, because doing so might create trouble
eg: I know he was wrong, but it happened months ago. Just let sleeping dogs lie
eg: She wanted to bring up the old disagreement, but I told her to let sleeping dogs lie

# Let bygones be bygones

def: To forgive and forget about past disagreements or offenses
eg: We've both made mistakes. Let's let bygones be bygones and move forward
eg: After years of not speaking, they decided to let bygones be bygones

# Wear your heart on your sleeve

def: To openly show your emotions rather than keeping them hidden
eg: She always wears her heart on her sleeve, so everyone knows when she's upset
eg: He wears his heart on his sleeve, which makes him a very honest but vulnerable person

# Get a taste of your own medicine

def: To experience the same unpleasant treatment that you have given to others
eg: He's always making fun of people, so he got a taste of his own medicine when they pranked him
eg: She never replies to messages, and now that no one's texting her back, she's getting a taste of her own medicine

# Cry wolf

def: To raise a false alarm so often that people stop believing you when there is a real problem
eg: He's cried wolf so many times about quitting that nobody takes him seriously anymore
eg: If you keep crying wolf about deadlines, no one will help when there's a real emergency

# Straight from the horse's mouth

def: Directly from the person who has direct knowledge of the matter; from the most reliable source
eg: I heard the news straight from the horse's mouth—the CEO himself told me
eg: Don't listen to rumors. I got this information straight from the horse's mouth

# You can't judge a book by its cover

def: You should not form an opinion about someone or something based only on their appearance
eg: He looked intimidating, but he turned out to be the nicest guy. You really can't judge a book by its cover
eg: The restaurant didn't look like much from the outside, but the food was incredible—you can't judge a book by its cover

# Read between the lines

def: To understand the hidden or implied meaning of something, rather than just the literal words
eg: She said she was "fine," but if you read between the lines, she was clearly upset
eg: The email seemed polite, but reading between the lines, they were not happy with our work

# Turn a blind eye

def: To pretend not to notice something, especially something wrong or illegal
eg: The manager turned a blind eye to employees leaving early on Fridays
eg: You can't just turn a blind eye to bullying in schools

# Think outside the box

def: To think creatively or in an unconventional way
eg: We need to think outside the box if we want to solve this problem
eg: The marketing team was encouraged to think outside the box for the new campaign

# Down to earth

def: Practical, realistic, and easy to talk to; not pretentious
eg: Despite being famous, she's very down to earth and treats everyone kindly
eg: I like working with him because he's so down to earth and straightforward

# The tip of the iceberg

def: Only a small and visible part of a much larger problem or situation
eg: The financial scandal we've uncovered is just the tip of the iceberg
eg: These complaints are just the tip of the iceberg; there are much bigger issues beneath the surface

# Get cold feet

def: To suddenly become too nervous or afraid to do something you had planned to do
eg: He was going to propose, but he got cold feet at the last minute
eg: She got cold feet right before the job interview and almost didn't go

# On cloud nine

def: Extremely happy; feeling wonderful
eg: She's been on cloud nine ever since she got accepted into her dream university
eg: When he found out he was going to be a father, he was on cloud nine

# In the heat of the moment

def: Overwhelmed by what is happening at that instant, often leading to impulsive actions or words
eg: I said some things I didn't mean in the heat of the moment
eg: In the heat of the moment, he made a decision he later regretted

# Keep your chin up

def: To remain cheerful and optimistic during a difficult time
eg: I know things are tough right now, but keep your chin up
eg: She kept her chin up through the whole recovery process

# In hot water

def: In trouble or in a difficult situation, especially because of something you have done
eg: He's in hot water with his boss for missing the deadline
eg: If you don't pay your taxes, you'll find yourself in hot water

# Get the ball rolling

def: To start a process or activity; to set things in motion

# Spill the beans

def: To reveal secret information, either accidentally or deliberately
eg: Come on, spill the beans! What did he say?
eg: Someone spilled the beans about the surprise party, and now she knows everything

# Put all your eggs in one basket

def: To risk everything by depending on one plan, one opportunity, or one source
eg: Don't put all your eggs in one basket; apply to several universities
eg: Investing all your savings in one stock is putting all your eggs in one basket

# Put your foot in your mouth

def: To say something embarrassing, inappropriate, or offensive without intending to
eg: I really put my foot in my mouth when I asked about her husband—they're divorced
eg: He put his foot in his mouth by accidentally revealing the surprise

# Turn over a new leaf

def: To make a fresh start and change your behavior for the better
eg: After years of bad habits, he decided to turn over a new leaf and start exercising
eg: She turned over a new leaf and started being more organized at work

# The whole nine yards

def: Everything possible; the full amount or extent

eg: For her birthday, he went the whole nine yards: dinner, flowers, and a surprise trip

eg: After the last warning, he knew he was on thin ice at work

# Caught red-handed

def: Caught in the act of doing something wrong or illegal

eg: I caught my brother red-handed eating the last piece of cake

# Every cloud has a silver lining

eg: He cheated on the test, so you cheated too? Two wrongs don't make a right

# When pigs fly

def: Something that will never happen; used to express disbelief or impossibility
eg: He'll clean his room when pigs fly
eg: You think she'll apologize? When pigs fly!

# A picture is worth a thousand words

def: An image can convey a message or meaning more effectively than a lengthy description

# Don't put the cart before the horse
