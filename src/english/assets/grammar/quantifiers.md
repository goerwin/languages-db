---
title: English Quantifiers
description: English quantifiers such as some, any, much, many, few, little, and more, with usage rules for countable and uncountable nouns
---

# some

use: In affirmative sentences with both countable and uncountable nouns
eg: I bought some apples at the store
eg: There is some water in the bottle

use: In questions when you expect a "yes" answer or when offering something
eg: Would you like some coffee?
eg: Can I have some more cake?

use: In requests to make them sound more polite
eg: Could you lend me some money?
eg: I need some help with this

tip: Use "some" instead of "any" in offers and polite requests even though they are questions
tip: "Some" suggests an unspecified but limited amount

# any

use: In questions with both countable and uncountable nouns
eg: Do you have any milk?
eg: Are there any students in the classroom?

use: In negative sentences to mean "none" or "zero"
eg: I don't have any time today
eg: There aren't any cookies left

use: With anyone, anything, anywhere for indefinite reference
eg: Has anyone seen my keys?
eg: I can't find anything to wear

use: In affirmative sentences meaning "it doesn't matter which"
eg: Take any seat you like
eg: Any student can answer this question

tip: "Any" in negatives often pairs with "not" or "never"
tip: In affirmative "any" emphasizes that the choice is unrestricted

# much

use: With uncountable nouns, mainly in questions and negatives
eg: How much sugar do you need?
eg: I don't have much time

use: In formal affirmative sentences
eg: Much effort was required to complete the task
eg: There is much to be done

use: With "too" or "so" for emphasis
eg: That's too much information
eg: I have so much work to do

tip: In informal affirmative, prefer "a lot of" instead of "much"
tip: "Much" is common with abstract nouns like time, money, work, advice

# many

use: With countable plural nouns, mainly in questions and negatives
eg: How many books did you read?
eg: There aren't many people here

use: In formal affirmative sentences
eg: Many students attended the lecture
eg: Many attempts have been made

use: With "too" or "so" for emphasis
eg: You have too many shoes
eg: So many things went wrong

tip: In informal affirmative, prefer "a lot of" instead of "many"
tip: "Many a" is a formal/literary form: "Many a student has failed this test"

# a lot of / lots of

use: With both countable and uncountable nouns in affirmative sentences
eg: I have a lot of friends
eg: She spends lots of time studying

use: Informal replacement for much and many in affirmative contexts
eg: There's a lot of traffic today
eg: Lots of people came to the party

use: Can appear in questions and negatives for emphasis
eg: Do you have a lot of work?
eg: I don't have lots of money

tip: "Lots of" is more informal than "a lot of"
tip: Both can be written as one word "alot" is incorrect; keep the space

# a few

use: With countable nouns to mean a small but positive number
eg: I have a few minutes to talk
eg: She bought a few apples

use: Suggests "some" or "enough" with a positive connotation
eg: We need a few more chairs
eg: There are a few good restaurants nearby

tip: "A few" implies the number is small but satisfactory
tip: "A few" can be modified: "quite a few" means a fairly large number

# few

use: With countable nouns to mean "not many" with negative connotation
eg: Few people understand quantum physics
eg: There are few opportunities in this town

use: Suggests "not enough" or scarcity
eg: Very few students passed the exam
eg: Few of us have seen that movie

tip: "Few" without "a" has a negative meaning; "a few" is positive
tip: "The few" means the small number that exist: "the few who survived"

# a little

use: With uncountable nouns to mean a small but positive amount
eg: Add a little salt to the soup
eg: I have a little money saved

use: Suggests "some" or "enough" with positive connotation
eg: We need a little more time
eg: There's a little hope left

tip: "A little" implies the amount is small but adequate
tip: "A little" can modify adjectives: "I'm a little tired"

# little

use: With uncountable nouns to mean "not much" with negative connotation
eg: Little progress was made
eg: There is little chance of success

use: Suggests "not enough" or scarcity
eg: Very little water remained
eg: Little of the food was edible

tip: "Little" without "a" has a negative meaning; "a little" is positive
tip: "The little" means the small amount that exists: "the little we have"

# enough

use: Before nouns to mean sufficient amount or number
eg: We have enough food for everyone
eg: Do you have enough chairs?

use: After adjectives and adverbs to mean sufficient degree
eg: This room is big enough
eg: She didn't run fast enough

use: As a pronoun when the noun is understood
eg: That's enough, thank you
eg: I've had enough of this

tip: "Enough" comes before the noun but after the adjective
tip: "Enough of" is used before determiners: "enough of the cake"

# too much / too many

use: Too much with uncountable nouns for excessive amount
eg: You're drinking too much coffee
eg: There's too much noise in here

use: Too many with countable plural nouns for excessive number
eg: You have too many books
eg: Too many people applied for the job

use: Too much can modify verbs or stand alone
eg: She talks too much
eg: That's too much

tip: "Too much" = more than needed or wanted; "too many" = more than the right number
tip: "Much too" + adjective means excessively: "much too expensive"

# plenty of

use: With countable and uncountable nouns to mean more than enough
eg: We have plenty of time
eg: There are plenty of options

use: Suggests abundance or adequacy
eg: Don't worry, there's plenty of food
eg: Plenty of people agree with you

use: Can stand alone as a pronoun
eg: We have plenty
eg: That's plenty, thanks

tip: "Plenty of" is always positive; it implies no shortage
tip: "Plenty" without "of" is used after "there is" or "we have"

# several

use: With countable nouns only, meaning more than two but not very many
eg: I've been there several times
eg: Several students asked questions

use: Suggests a number between a few and many
eg: We need several more volunteers
eg: Several of the guests left early

tip: "Several" cannot be used with uncountable nouns
tip: "Several" is more than "a few" but less than "many"

# each

use: Referring to every individual one separately, one by one
eg: Each student received a certificate
eg: I gave each of them a present

use: Emphasizes individuality within a group
eg: Each day brings new challenges
eg: They each have their own room

use: With "of" before plural nouns or pronouns
eg: Each of the books is different
eg: Each of us must try

tip: "Each" takes a singular verb when it is the subject
tip: "Each" emphasizes the individual; "every" emphasizes the whole group

# every

use: Referring to all members of a group as a whole
eg: Every student passed the exam
eg: I go to the gym every day

use: Suggests regularity or completeness
eg: Every time I see her, she smiles
eg: We checked every room

use: In expressions like every other, every single
eg: I see him every other week
eg: Every single person voted

tip: "Every" is used with singular countable nouns
tip: "Every" emphasizes the group as a whole; "each" emphasizes individuals

# all

use: Meaning everything or everyone, the whole quantity
eg: All students must attend
eg: I've read all the books

use: With "of" before determiners or pronouns
eg: All of the money was stolen
eg: All of us are tired

use: Without "of" before plural nouns or uncountable nouns
eg: All children need love
eg: All the water has evaporated

tip: "All" can be used with countable and uncountable nouns
tip: "All" + singular noun can mean "every": "all day" = the whole day

# both

use: Referring to two things or people together
eg: Both parents attended the meeting
eg: I like both options

use: With "of" before determiners or pronouns
eg: Both of my brothers are doctors
eg: Both of them agreed

use: In structures like "both...and"
eg: She is both smart and kind
eg: Both the cat and the dog are sleeping

tip: "Both" always refers to exactly two items
tip: "Both" takes a plural verb

# either

use: Meaning one or the other of two options
eg: You can sit on either side
eg: Either option works for me

use: In "either...or" to present alternatives
eg: Either come in or go out
eg: You can have either tea or coffee

use: In negative contexts meaning "neither"
eg: I don't like either of them
eg: She didn't choose either option

tip: "Either" is used with singular nouns and singular verb in "either...or" when both subjects are singular
tip: "Either of" is followed by plural noun but can take singular or plural verb

# neither

use: Meaning not one and not the other of two
eg: Neither answer is correct
eg: Neither parent was present

use: In "neither...nor" to join two negatives
eg: Neither the cat nor the dog was hungry
eg: I neither know nor care

use: As a short response
eg: Which one do you want? Neither
eg: Neither of them came

tip: "Neither" takes a singular verb when it is the subject
tip: "Neither...nor" can take singular or plural verb depending on the closest subject

# no / none

use: No before a noun to mean zero quantity
eg: There is no milk left
eg: I have no idea

use: None as a pronoun when no noun follows
eg: How many cookies? None
eg: None of the students passed

use: No with both countable and uncountable nouns
eg: No problem
eg: There are no tickets available

tip: "No" = "not any"; use "no" before a noun, "none" when the noun is omitted
tip: "None of" + plural can take singular or plural verb; singular is more formal

# whole

use: Meaning entire or complete, before singular countable nouns
eg: I ate the whole pizza
eg: The whole building was destroyed

use: Emphasizing completeness of a single unit
eg: She spent the whole day studying
eg: The whole story is a lie

use: With "the" before the noun
eg: I read the whole book
eg: The whole world is watching

tip: "Whole" comes after "the" or other determiners
tip: "The whole of" is used before proper nouns or when the noun needs "of": "the whole of London"

# some vs. any

use: Use some in affirmative sentences and in questions when offering or expecting yes
eg: I need some help
eg: Would you like some tea?

use: Use any in questions when the answer is unknown and in negative sentences
eg: Do you have any questions?
eg: I don't have any money

use: In if-clauses, both can appear; any is more common
eg: If you have any problems, call me
eg: If you need some help, just ask

tip: The rule "some in positive, any in negative and questions" has exceptions for offers and requests
tip: "Any" in affirmatives means "it doesn't matter which"; "some" means an unspecified amount

# much / many vs. a lot of

use: Much and many are preferred in questions and negatives
eg: How much does it cost?
eg: I don't have many friends

use: A lot of is preferred in informal affirmative sentences
eg: I have a lot of work
eg: There are a lot of people here

use: Much and many in affirmative sound formal or literary
eg: Much has been said about this
eg: Many would disagree

tip: In spoken English, "a lot of" replaces "much" and "many" in affirmatives
tip: "Much" and "many" are still correct in affirmatives but sound more formal

# a few vs. few / a little vs. little

use: A few and a little have positive meaning; few and little have negative meaning
eg: I have a few ideas (positive: some ideas)
eg: I have few ideas (negative: not many ideas)

use: A little with uncountable, a few with countable
eg: We need a little more time
eg: We need a few more minutes

use: The article "a" makes the critical difference
eg: A little hope remains (some hope)
eg: Little hope remains (almost no hope)

tip: "Few" and "little" without "a" suggest insufficiency or scarcity
tip: "Very few" and "very little" strengthen the negative meaning

# each vs. every

use: Each emphasizes individual items one by one
eg: Each person must sign the form
eg: I spoke to each of them

use: Every emphasizes the group as a whole or regularity
eg: Every student passed
eg: I go there every week

use: Each can refer to two or more; every refers to three or more
eg: Each of the two options is valid
eg: Every one of the five teams played

tip: "Each" can be used with "of" and plural pronouns; "every" cannot
tip: "Every" is used with singular nouns; "each" can emphasize distribution

# all vs. every vs. each

use: All refers to the group as a whole or total quantity
eg: All students must register
eg: I've lost all my keys

use: Every refers to each member of a group, used with singular nouns
eg: Every student has a locker
eg: Every room was checked

use: Each emphasizes individual items separately
eg: Each student received a different grade
eg: I gave each child a toy

tip: "All" + plural noun; "every" and "each" + singular noun
tip: "All" can mean "the whole of" with uncountable: "all the water"

# common mistakes

use: Wrong quantifier with countable vs uncountable nouns
eg: Wrong: much people. Right: many people
eg: Wrong: many water. Right: much water

use: Using "any" in affirmative when "some" is meant
eg: Wrong: I have any money. Right: I have some money
eg: Wrong: Give me any sugar. Right: Give me some sugar (when requesting)

use: Confusing few/little with a few/a little
eg: Wrong: I have few time. Right: I have a little time (positive)
eg: Wrong: Few people came (if you mean some came). Right: A few people came

tip: Countable nouns use many, few, a few, several; uncountable use much, little, a little
tip: Check whether you mean positive (enough) or negative (not enough) when choosing few vs a few
