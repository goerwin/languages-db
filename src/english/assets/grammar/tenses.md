---
title: English Tenses
description: All 12 English tenses plus "going to" future, with structures, usage rules, examples, and time markers for each
---

# present simple

form: Subject + base verb (+ -s/-es for he/she/it)
neg: Subject + do/does + not + base verb
q: Do/Does + subject + base verb + ?

use: For habits, routines, and things that happen regularly
eg: She goes to the gym every morning
eg: I usually drink coffee after lunch

use: For general truths and facts
eg: Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius
eg: The sun rises in the east

use: For permanent situations
eg: He works at a bank
eg: They live in New York

use: For scheduled events and timetables
eg: The train leaves at 9 AM tomorrow
eg: The conference starts on Monday

tip: Add -s or -es to the verb for he/she/it (works, goes, watches)
tip: Use "do/does" for questions and negatives, not the main verb
tip: Common time markers: always, usually, often, sometimes, rarely, never, every day/week/month

# present continuous

form: Subject + am/is/are + verb-ing
neg: Subject + am/is/are + not + verb-ing
q: Am/Is/Are + subject + verb-ing + ?

use: For actions happening right now, at the moment of speaking
eg: She is reading a book right now
eg: I'm waiting for the bus

use: For temporary situations (not permanent)
eg: He is staying with his parents this week
eg: They are living in Paris for six months

use: For future arrangements (already planned)
eg: We are meeting at 5 PM tomorrow
eg: She is flying to London next week

use: For annoying habits (with always, constantly)
eg: He is always interrupting me
eg: She is constantly complaining about the weather

tip: Use present continuous for actions in progress, not present simple
tip: Stative verbs (know, believe, want, like) usually don't use continuous forms
tip: Common time markers: now, at the moment, right now, currently, this week/month

# present perfect

form: Subject + have/has + past participle
neg: Subject + have/has + not + past participle
q: Have/Has + subject + past participle + ?

use: For past actions with present results or relevance
eg: I have lost my keys (I still don't have them)
eg: She has broken her leg (it's still broken)

use: For life experiences (when time is not specified)
eg: I have been to Japan three times
eg: He has never tried sushi

use: For actions that started in the past and continue to now
eg: She has worked here since 2010
eg: We have known each other for ten years

use: For recent completed actions (with just, already, yet)
eg: I have just finished my homework
eg: Have you eaten yet?

tip: Use "have been" not "have gone" when the person has returned (I have been to Paris)
tip: Use "for" with duration and "since" with a starting point
tip: Common time markers: just, already, yet, ever, never, since, for, so far, recently

# present perfect continuous

form: Subject + have/has + been + verb-ing
neg: Subject + have/has + not + been + verb-ing
q: Have/Has + subject + been + verb-ing + ?

use: For actions that started in the past and continue to now (emphasis on duration)
eg: She has been studying for three hours
eg: They have been living here since 2015

use: For recently finished actions with visible results
eg: Your hands are dirty. Have you been gardening?
eg: She is tired because she has been working all day

use: For repeated actions over a period of time
eg: I have been going to the gym every week
eg: He has been writing articles for this magazine

use: For temporary situations (with emphasis on the ongoing nature)
eg: We have been staying at a hotel while our house is being renovated
eg: She has been feeling unwell lately

tip: Often interchangeable with present perfect, but continuous emphasizes duration and process
tip: Stative verbs (know, believe, love) are not used in continuous form
tip: Common time markers: for, since, all day/week, how long, lately, recently

# past simple

form: Subject + past tense verb (regular: -ed / irregular: varies)
neg: Subject + did + not + base verb
q: Did + subject + base verb + ?

use: For completed actions at a specific time in the past
eg: I saw him yesterday
eg: She left the office at 6 PM

use: For a series of completed actions in the past
eg: He woke up, had breakfast, and went to work
eg: They arrived, unpacked, and explored the city

use: For past habits or states (that no longer exist)
eg: When I was young, I played tennis every weekend
eg: She lived in Berlin for five years

use: For past facts or generalizations
eg: The Romans built many roads
eg: Shakespeare wrote many plays

tip: Use past simple when the time is stated or clearly implied
tip: Irregular verbs have unique past forms (go-went, see-saw, write-wrote)
tip: Common time markers: yesterday, last week/month/year, ago, in 2020, when I was young

# past continuous

form: Subject + was/were + verb-ing
neg: Subject + was/were + not + verb-ing
q: Was/Were + subject + verb-ing + ?

use: For actions in progress at a specific moment in the past
eg: I was watching TV when the phone rang
eg: She was cooking dinner at 8 PM last night

use: For interrupted actions (one action interrupted by another)
eg: They were having lunch when the fire alarm went off
eg: He was driving home when he had an accident

use: For two simultaneous actions in the past
eg: While I was cooking, she was setting the table
eg: He was reading while she was writing

use: For background description in a story
eg: The sun was shining and birds were singing
eg: People were walking in the park and children were playing

tip: Use past continuous for the longer action, past simple for the shorter interrupting action
tip: "While" often introduces past continuous; "when" can introduce either tense
tip: Common time markers: at that moment, at 5 PM, when, while, all day yesterday

# past perfect

form: Subject + had + past participle
neg: Subject + had + not + past participle
q: Had + subject + past participle + ?

use: For an action completed before another past action
eg: She had already left when I arrived
eg: They had finished dinner before we got there

use: For an action completed before a specific time in the past
eg: By 2020, he had published five books
eg: I had never been abroad before last year

use: For reported speech (when the original was present perfect or past simple)
eg: She said she had seen the movie
eg: He told me he had lost his job

use: For unreal past conditions (third conditional)
eg: If I had known, I would have helped
eg: She would have passed if she had studied harder

tip: Past perfect shows the earlier of two past actions
tip: Often used with "before," "after," "by the time," "already," "never"
tip: Common time markers: before, after, by the time, already, never, just, when

# past perfect continuous

form: Subject + had + been + verb-ing
neg: Subject + had + not + been + verb-ing
q: Had + subject + been + verb-ing + ?

use: For actions that were in progress before another past action
eg: She had been working for two hours when he called
eg: They had been waiting for 30 minutes before the bus arrived

use: For the cause of something in the past
eg: His hands were dirty because he had been gardening
eg: She was tired because she had been running

use: For repeated actions over a period before a point in the past
eg: I had been trying to reach him all day
eg: They had been arguing before we arrived

use: For duration of an action up to a point in the past
eg: How long had you been living there before you moved?
eg: She had been teaching for 20 years when she retired

tip: Emphasizes duration and continuity before another past event
tip: Often interchangeable with past perfect, but continuous stresses the ongoing process
tip: Common time markers: for, since, how long, before, when, all day/week

# future simple (will)

form: Subject + will + base verb
neg: Subject + will + not + base verb
q: Will + subject + base verb + ?

use: For spontaneous decisions made at the moment of speaking
eg: I'll have a coffee, please
eg: It's cold. I'll close the window

use: For predictions and beliefs about the future
eg: I think it will rain tomorrow
eg: She will probably be late

use: For promises, offers, and refusals
eg: I will help you with your homework
eg: I won't tell anyone your secret

use: For future facts and things we cannot control
eg: The sun will rise at 6 AM tomorrow
eg: She will be 30 next month

tip: Use "will" for instant decisions, not for plans made before
tip: "Will" is often used with: I think, I'm sure, I expect, probably, perhaps
tip: Common time markers: tomorrow, next week/month/year, in the future, soon, later

# future continuous

form: Subject + will + be + verb-ing
neg: Subject + will + not + be + verb-ing
q: Will + subject + be + verb-ing + ?

use: For actions that will be in progress at a specific time in the future
eg: This time tomorrow, I will be flying to Paris
eg: At 8 PM tonight, she will be having dinner

use: For planned or expected events in the future
eg: We will be staying at the Hilton
eg: They will be arriving next Tuesday

use: For polite questions about someone's plans
eg: Will you be using the car tonight?
eg: Will you be needing any help?

use: For actions that will happen as a matter of course
eg: I'll be seeing him at the meeting anyway
eg: She'll be passing the post office, so she can mail it

tip: Emphasizes that an action will be in progress, not just that it will happen
tip: Often used for arrangements that are already fixed
tip: Common time markers: at this time tomorrow, at 5 PM, next week, in an hour

# future perfect

form: Subject + will + have + past participle
neg: Subject + will + not + have + past participle
q: Will + subject + have + past participle + ?

use: For actions that will be completed before a specific time in the future
eg: By next year, I will have graduated from university
eg: She will have finished the report by Friday

use: For actions completed before another future action
eg: I will have left before you arrive
eg: They will have eaten by the time we get there

use: For assumptions about the past from a future perspective
eg: You will have heard the news by now
eg: They will have received our letter by tomorrow

use: For duration that will be complete at a future point
eg: By June, we will have been married for 10 years
eg: Next month she will have worked here for five years

tip: Always use "by" or "by the time" to indicate the deadline
tip: The action is viewed as complete from a future viewpoint
tip: Common time markers: by, by the time, by then, before, in a year

# future perfect continuous

form: Subject + will + have + been + verb-ing
neg: Subject + will + not + have + been + verb-ing
q: Will + subject + have + been + verb-ing + ?

use: For actions that will have been in progress for a duration by a future time
eg: By next month, I will have been studying English for five years
eg: At midnight, she will have been working for 12 hours

use: For the cause of a future result
eg: She will be tired because she will have been traveling all day
eg: By the end of the week, he will have been running every day

use: For emphasis on duration of an ongoing future action
eg: How long will you have been living there by December?
eg: They will have been waiting for over an hour when we arrive

use: For actions that started in the past and continue to a future point
eg: In 2026, we will have been married for 20 years
eg: By the time she retires, she will have been teaching for 35 years

tip: Emphasizes the duration of an action up to a future point
tip: Often used with "for" to show how long
tip: Common time markers: by, by the time, for, when, by then, in + time period

# going to (future)

form: Subject + am/is/are + going to + base verb
neg: Subject + am/is/are + not + going to + base verb
q: Am/Is/Are + subject + going to + base verb + ?

use: For plans and intentions (decided before speaking)
eg: I'm going to study medicine at university
eg: We're going to get married next year

use: For predictions based on present evidence
eg: Look at those clouds. It's going to rain
eg: She's going to have a baby (she's pregnant)

use: For imminent actions (about to happen)
eg: Get back! The tree is going to fall
eg: I'm going to be sick

use: For decisions already made (contrast with will for spontaneous decisions)
eg: I've decided. I'm going to quit my job
eg: They're going to buy a new car

tip: Use "going to" for plans; use "will" for spontaneous decisions
tip: "Going to" is common in spoken English; "will" is more formal in some contexts
tip: Common time markers: tomorrow, next week, this weekend, soon, in the future
